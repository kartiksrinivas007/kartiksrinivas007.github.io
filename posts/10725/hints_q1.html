Q. What is this problem about?

A. Check whether an arbitrary polynomial is always positive or not.

Q. What is \(x_{\alpha}\) with \(\alpha = (\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \ldots \alpha_d)\)?

A. \(x_{\alpha}\) is literally just a type of moment of a distribution \(P\). Sample some random vector \(X\) over \(P\), pick the \(d\) entries of \(X\), raise each entry to its corresponding power \(\alpha_i\) and multiply them all together to obtain \(\prod X_i^{\alpha_i}\). This is a scalar random value for each random \(X \sim P\). The expected value of this scalar random variable is denoted as \(x_{\alpha}\). In this post, I call such \(x_{\alpha}\) “general moments”.

Hints for Part 1

Q. What is p?

A. \(p\) is just a polynomial over \(d\) variables. An example with max degree \(t = 2\) and \(d = 2\) variables \(x_1, x_2\) would be of the form \[ p(x) = \sum_{\alpha} \lambda_{\alpha} x_{\alpha} = \lambda_1x_1^2 + \lambda_2x_2^2 + \lambda_3 x_1x_2 + \lambda_4x_1 + \lambda_5x_2 + \lambda_6 = \lambda^T J(x) \] Think of \(x_2^2\) to be \(x_1^0x_2^2\) = \(x_{(0, 2)}\) and \(\lambda_2 = \lambda_{(2, 0)}\)

\(J(x)\) being a vector carrying terms of the form \(\prod x_i^{\alpha_i}\).

Here are two interesting questions to ask that might help you:-

  • If p(x) is always positive(for example, it may be the square of some other polynomial of degree \(t/2\)), what would happen when I sample any random vector \(X \sim P\) and plug it into the polynomial p to obtain P(X)?, what does that tell me about \(\mathbb{E}[p(X)]\)?. What is each entry of \(J(X)\)?

So instead of imposing the condition that you obtained in part 1 for every \(P\) and \(q\), impose it for all possible values of \(x_{\alpha}\), and all choices of the polynomial \(q = p(x)^2\) (the latter means all choices of \(\lambda\)).
